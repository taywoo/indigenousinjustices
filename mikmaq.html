    Mi'kmaq
    Lobster Dispute

    21 years after Canada’s Supreme Court affirmed their right to fish for a moderate livelihood, Mi’kmaq nations are still fighting for their rights to be upheld.

    What Happened?

    Early October flames engulfed a fishing boat belonging to a Mi’kmaw fisherman from the Sipekne’katik First Nation at a Nova Scotia wharf.

    The suspicious fire is the latest in a weeks-long saga of conflict between Indigenous lobster fishers and non-Indigenous commercial fishers. In fact, the conflict dates back to the 18th c. when the treaty was first created. Then in 1999, the Supreme Court of Canada affirmed the treaty rights of the Mi’kmaq to earn a moderate livlihood after a similar series of events occured leading to Indigenous communities facing violent racist backlash.

    Mi'kmaq are once again having to fight for their right and demand the government of Canada uphold their end of the bargain. While facing violence and racist outburts from Commercial fishers in Nova Scotia.

    The core of this dispute was never about sustainability* It's covert racism.

    As of right now, there are 10 Sipekne’katik vessels, 50 traps per vessel, a total of 500 traps. Compare that to the commercial sector, for a total of about 35,000 traps. There is no evidence to suggest that this would be problematic

    The real issue is that the RCMP stood by and did nothing while their property was raided and set ablaze. Who cares about a few lobsters? Their lives were at stake. However, it's worth noting that the RCMP was initially created to control and police Indigenous people so I guess they're just doing their job?
